# Remix

Models collapse toward the single most typical answer. Remix counters that: instead of returning the one stereotypical response, consider the real spread of valid answers and pick across it — common answers still appear often, but uncommon-yet-valid ones surface at their honest rate instead of being flattened to zero.

## When to use

- A list of "options" is really one idea in five outfits.
- Names, examples, personas, or approaches are all landing in the same obvious register.
- The user asks for non-obvious, varied, or fresh directions.
- A Luckiest plan task would benefit from breadth before committing.

## How to remix

1. **Name the typical answer first.** Say what the safe, default response would be — the one everyone reaches for. This is the thing to move off of, and naming it stops you returning it by accident.
2. **Map the space, not the peak.** Sketch the actual range of valid answers along a real axis (audience, mechanism, tone, risk, medium). The goal is the distribution, not the mode.
3. **Sample proportionally, not uniformly.** Keep one or two common answers — they are common because they often fit. Then pull genuinely different ones from across the space at their natural rate. Variety is not "five weird ideas"; it is an honest cross-section.
4. **Pressure-test the outliers.** An uncommon option earns its place only if it is actually valid for the task. Drop the ones that are different for difference's sake.

## Output

Return a spread that visibly spans the space: label what each option trades off, so the user sees why they differ rather than just that they differ. Lead with the strongest, not the safest.

## Anti-patterns

- Do not manufacture novelty — a bizarre option that does not fit the task is noise, not variety.
- Do not discard the common answer on principle; sometimes the typical response is the right one, and remix keeps it in the mix at its real weight.
- Do not return five variations of one idea and call it variety. If they share the same axis value, they are one option.
